Qtum Phantom Protocol: Unveiling Layer-1 Privacy

Qtum
Follow
Mar 27, 2020 · 10 min read

Just how cool are blockchain-based privacy implementations?
Article translated & reprinted from Odaily.

Author: Huang Xue Jiao

On February 13, 2020, Qtum released the white paper for a Layer-1 zk-SNARKs-based privacy protocol named Qtum Phantom.

Odaily has learned that the Phantom privacy protocol is not only a key project for Qtum this year, but is also one of the few privacy solutions in China.

Focusing on Phantom and its zero-knowledge proof algorithm, Odaily and Yi Zheng, the chief developer of Qtum China, had an in-depth discussion.

As Yi Zheng said, most of the people’s understanding of privacy focuses on privacy coins and their representative technologies. Few people think about how much we need privacy. If there is a demand for privacy on public blockchains, then why isn’t there a privacy technology standard?

From the design of Phantom, we can see the real motivation of privacy developers, their consideration for selecting different privacy technologies, as well as privacy implementations and bottlenecks.

What Does Privacy Mean to You?

Public Chain Privacy

Qtum’s interest in privacy developed in 2019. At the beginning of the year, the market favored Grin and Beam of the MimbleWimble protocol. One month later, the Litecoin Foundation announced that it would work with the Beam team to implement MW on Litecoin to enhance the privacy of transactions.

Faster payments were the biggest innovation of Litecoin. Adding privacy options allows users’ account balances to be hidden and transfers hidden, which is indeed a further improvement of the user experience. But for second-generation public blockchains like Qtum and Ethereum, what is the significance of building Layer 1 privacy?

Yi Zheng put forward two reasons. First, Qtum and its currency needed to enhance privacy. The Qtum blockchain was launched in 2017 and is compatible with the Bitcoin transaction model and the Ethereum virtual machine. This allowed Qtum to provide support for smart contracts with a logically simple, stable, and secure underlying architecture.

The addition of the Layer 1 privacy layer is not meant to “enable” smart contracts, but to enhance currency functions. In the two years since its launch, using smart contracts to be a “world computer” like Ethereum is still gaining acceptance. Expecting that the near-term transformation of the world through smart contracts has been less viable, Qtum has revisited the platforms “currency function”.

Many people in the industry hold the view that the best substitute for traditional fiat banknotes is privacy currencies, not either electronic money or ordinary encrypted digital currencies on blockchains with public account balances such as Bitcoin.

In the era of mobile payments, electronic money has accelerated to replace traditional banknotes, but at the same time, centralized platforms monetize personal privacy, and the digital traces of a person’s life are permanently saved. Anyone who the power, for good or evil, can access the transaction details of your personal life.

“The death of cash is the death of freedom.” Some even shouted such slogans and expressed hope for private currencies.

And Qtum is one of them. Yi Zheng revealed that after implementing Phantom, Qtum will also provide an open-source protocol similar to algorithmic stable coins (typically DAI), allowing users to freely create highly anonymous stable coins while making Qtum a more powerful currency issue platform.

Another reason why Qtum values privacy is that privacy is one of the “high-tech” areas in the blockchain which has great potential.

Just like cryptography before Bitcoin became famous, privacy technology has yet to be a broader benchmark product. But this cannot deny its potential to become a fundamental component of the blockchain world.

Mastering this future technology is very attractive for a public chain such as Qtum that has been operating stably for several years and is looking for a breakthrough.

However, as Yi Zheng said, due to technical difficulties, limited development resources (researchers), and the unknown market space, privacy has not yet achieved a large technological leap forward. Qtum hopes that Phantom’s attempt will fill a gap in domestic privacy practices to a certain extent.

Improvements Based on zk-SNARKs

zk-SNARKs, which has been continuously improved and promoted since it was proposed in 2014, has now become one of the most mainstream privacy technologies in the blockchain world. Phantom has inherited zk-SNARKs and added some effective innovations.

According to Yi Zheng, there are three main reasons for choosing zk-SNARKs.

First, among various privacy technologies, zk-SNARKs has the strongest anonymity. It can hide both the transaction amount and the transaction address, making it impossible to trace the connection between transactions.

As shown in the table, MimbleWimble, as a cutting-edge zero-knowledge proof algorithm, has the same anonymity as zk-SNARK. Therefore, Qtum also considered adopting the MW scheme, has done a lot of research with the Beam team, and released a white paper for the Layer 2 MW scheme. However, because there were no senior MimbleWimble researchers in China, MW was shelved in favor of the earlier zk-SNARK.

According to Yi Zheng Yi, there are currently two Phantom developers, both from the Qtum community. Their solution first appeared in a privacy technology hackathon held by Qtum last year, and was later funded by Qtum and jointly developed by both parties.

Back to the technology of zk-SNARKs, many people take it for granted as zero-knowledge proof, but it is not. In cryptography, zero-knowledge proofs refers to a type of “problem” like “Byzantine General”, and zk-SNARKs are one of them.

I believe many people are no stranger to the concept of zero-knowledge proof. Zero-Knowledge Proof, proposed in the 1980s, refers to a prover who proves to a verifier and makes them believe that they know or own something, but during the certification process, they cannot disclose anything about the underlying information.

In 2014 the Israeli Institute of Technology researchers Ben-Sasson and others applied this verification idea to digital currencies, and the anonymous transaction scheme supported by the zk-SNARKs algorithm was officially born.

Let ’s start with the lineage. There are two types of tokens specified in Phantom, one is a public token (that is, an ordinary QRC20 token), and the other is a hidden token.

The process of implementing private transactions with zk-SNARKs is roughly the same. When Alice wants to transfer QTUM to Bob anonymously, she needs to exchange the QTUM for a privacy token, let’s say zk-QTUM. She then initiates a transaction request to the system. The system will then use algorithms to generate a set of parameters for Alice’s transaction; then, the verifier (that is, the Proof of Stake node on Qtum) will verify this set of parameters.

When this set of parameters meets the requirements set by the system, the verifier executes Alice’s request and transfers her zk-QTUM to Bob’s account, but this result is not recorded on the chain and the blockchain on publishes Alice’s transaction information parameters (also called “proof”) and the result of the verifier verification.

Therefore, no one knows the specific transaction information except the two parties to the transaction, but the validity of the transaction is guaranteed.

The charm of cryptography lies in this. It can generate specific parameters based on Alice’s unique information, such as at what time and with whom. The transactions initiated, just like a randomly generated private key, make it difficult for an attacker to brute force cryptographic algorithms. The method deciphers the parameters or imitates the parameters recognized by the system, so although the transaction is not publicized, there are no problems.

Phantom Innovation

Phantom’s improvements to zk-SNARKs are mainly focused on the fact that less gas is spent on anonymous exchanges and the time to create transactions is shorter.

First, Phantom uses the Shrubs Merkel tree to store “commitment tickets” to improve efficiency.

In zk-SNARKs’ solution, the “note” represents an encrypted amount and recipient of the anonymous asset. The “commitment” is a unique hash generated by the system through a trapdoor function based on the note information for the verifier to verify the transaction without viewing the original data.

After the commitment is generated, it is stored in the Merkel tree. For each additional transaction on the chain, this tree needs to add a leaf node to store its commitments, so the entire Merkel tree needs to continuously update the state, consuming a large number of calculations.

Phantom innovatively introduces a structure called Node Merkel tree, which turns the data and hash values recorded on the original tree into the root node of multiple subtrees (Node) and gives multiple children to build a Merkel tree.

In this way, when you need to add a new node, you only need to change the root of the tree closest to it, without updating the state of the entire tree.

Node Merkel Tree

“This is a space-for-time idea, that is, increasing the storage space of the tree in exchange for a lower amount of calculation when updating the tree,” Yi Zheng concluded.

Phantom’s second innovation is to add an on-chain encrypted transmission method called In-band Secret (on-chain secret distribution) to zk-SNARKs.

In the original solution, because the address and amount of the anonymous transaction were not disclosed on the chain, the receiver of the transaction could not see the update of their account from the chain. To solve this problem, the sender of the transaction usually needs to send the transaction information (including the unencrypted address and amount) to the receiver through an off-chain method.

The sending method can be ancient and manual. For example, a screenshot could be transmitted through WeChat and other social platforms, or the receiver tells the sender an IP address for the sender to connect. After obtaining the original transaction information, the receiver can use it to construct the transaction and spend the funds transferred to them.

To achieve anonymous transactions, zk-SNARKs introduced primitive and tedious operations off the chain.

On-chain secret distribution technology can make the whole process on-chain and efficient. The implementation idea is to support the sender to encrypt the transaction information with the key and upload it to the chain. At the same time, the key can be transmitted to the chain using key sharing. The recovered transaction key can restore the original transaction data.

Through on-chain secret distribution, the sender only needs to know the relevant public key of the receiver to complete a transaction, and no longer needs to perform offline interaction with the receiver, which improves the overall efficiency.

Least “Sexy” Technology

Regarding the future of privacy, the potential is huge, but as Yi Zheng Yi said, due to technical difficulties, limited development resources (researchers), unknown market space, and other issues, privacy has not yet achieved a major technological breakthrough.

Privacy projects that are improved based on zero-knowledge proof algorithms also face the “privacy impossible triangle” of privacy, availability, and scalability.

The issue of usability is the most prominent, which is reflected in the high cost of privacy transactions (mainly transaction costs).

As shown below, among all types of zero-knowledge proof algorithms, the Σ-Bullets technology developed by the Aztec Protocol is the least expensive for privacy transactions, 0.9 Million (Gas Used) * 5 GWei (current Gas Price) = 0.0045 ETH (in ETH 200 US dollars, the transaction cost can reach 3 times that of ordinary transactions; while for the rest of the algorithms, each privacy transaction costs even 3–8 times that of Aztec.

The high cost of use will inevitably make budding privacy technologies scare many potential adopters. This is almost a situation where privacy is difficult to change.

What’s even more pessimistic is that teams like Aztec, who can propose highly innovative algorithms, are rare in the world. Among the teams that overcome the privacy problem, there are many projects such as Zether, which were jointly developed by Stanford researchers and Visa research department, but are still in the stage of experimental demonstration; schemes such as zk-STARKs, PLONK, which are considered to be zk-SNARKs evolution versions, are still in early testing.

In this regard, Yi Zheng believes that some privacy developers may have fallen into an excessive pursuit of the rigor of the theory, which took too long on some super difficult problems, which made the progress slow or even difficult.

Regarding the “impossible triangle” that is difficult to achieve, Yi Zheng believes that it can be solved through economic incentives. Just like the design of Bitcoin makes it faced with the possibility of being double-spent at any time, but Satoshi Nakamoto has set the mining algorithm to make people who acquire this ability pay a huge price, thereby minimizing this event. Probability, rather than wait until the issue is completely resolved before launching Bitcoin.

In addition to the existing developers’ pursuit of perfect interpretation, Yi Zheng believes that another major obstacle to the development of privacy technology is that, because it is so complex, the projects in the speculation market are rarely involved. The call for cross-chain is high, not because people’s demand for cross-chain has exploded, but more because it quickly gained market as a story.

Without a demand story, privacy naturally lacks the corresponding attention, and thus enters a cycle of lack of development resources.

Optimistically, compared with the past, there are more and more privacy projects now, and the use scenarios are no longer limited to privacy transactions, but cover privacy smart contracts and the use of privacy technologies to ensure data integrity and security.

For example, Filecoin uses zk-SNARKs technology to help validators verify that miners have indeed copied and saved user files. In this process, the miner only needs to upload a concise certificate to the chain and does not need to publish the specific information of the stored file, thereby effectively protecting user privacy.

According to Yi Zheng, Filecoin’s mining mechanism has led many practitioners who want to profit from mining to actively research zk-SNARKs.

In this step-by-step adoption, privacy technology, which is an important cornerstone of the blockchain world, may usher in a day of network effects.
